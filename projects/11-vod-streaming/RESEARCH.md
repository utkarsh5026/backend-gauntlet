# Video-on-Demand Streaming Internals: A Systems Engineer's Deep Dive

## TL;DR
- **VOD streaming is fundamentally a caching-and-adaptation problem, not a video problem.** The whole stack exists to solve one thing: deliver a multi-gigabyte file over a lossy, variable-bandwidth network to thousands of device types without the viewer noticing the network. Every design decision — codecs, segmentation, ABR, CDNs, HTTP-over-TCP — is downstream of that constraint. The winning architecture is "chop pre-encoded video into small HTTP-cacheable files, describe them in a text manifest, and let the client adapt," which is why HLS/DASH over HTTP beat stateful push protocols like RTMP/RTSP.
- **The pipeline is: ingest → transcode into a bitrate ladder → package into fMP4/CMAF segments → encrypt (CENC) → distribute via CDN → adapt on the client (ABR over MSE/EME).** Each stage is independently optimizable, and the biggest engineering wins of the last decade (per-title/per-shot encoding, CMAF unifying HLS+DASH, AV1, ISP-embedded caching, QUIC) are refinements within this frame.
- **The three defining trade-offs are compression-efficiency-vs-compute (codecs), quality-vs-rebuffering-vs-startup-latency (ABR), and cache-hit-vs-cost (CDN).** Netflix, YouTube, and Twitch make different choices because their economics differ: Netflix encodes few titles watched millions of times (spend compute lavishly), YouTube encodes billions of videos watched few times each (build custom ASICs), Twitch/live trades latency for everything.

## Key Findings

1. **Raw video is absurdly large; compression is non-optional.** Uncompressed 1080p60 4:2:0 8-bit runs on the order of 1.5 Gbps; a single minute of 4K60 exceeds 1 TB uncompressed. Codecs achieve 100–1000× compression by exploiting spatial redundancy (DCT + quantization), temporal redundancy (motion-compensated inter-frame prediction), and perceptual limits (chroma subsampling, psychovisual quantization).
2. **Codec choice is an economic decision, not just a technical one.** H.264 is the universal floor (plays everywhere, worst compression); HEVC gives ~50% savings but has a fragmented, expensive patent-pool landscape that kept it off the open web; AV1 is royalty-free and ~30% better than HEVC but historically ~10–50× slower to encode; VVC/H.266 is best-in-class efficiency but effectively absent from streaming due to licensing and encode cost.
3. **Per-title/per-shot encoding is the single biggest bandwidth win Netflix has published.** Instead of a fixed bitrate ladder, Netflix computes a convex hull of quality-vs-bitrate per title (later per shot), driven by the VMAF perceptual metric. Per the Netflix Technology Blog's dynamic-optimizer work (Ioannis Katsavounidis), "dynamic optimization enabled Netflix to reduce the bitrate of x264, VP9 (libvpx), and x265 by 28.04%, 37.61%, and 33.51%, respectively, while retaining the same quality."
4. **CMAF ended the HLS-vs-DASH duplicate-encoding tax.** By standardizing fMP4 segments with `cbcs` encryption, one set of media bytes now serves FairPlay (via HLS) and Widevine/PlayReady (via DASH), halving storage and doubling CDN cache efficiency.
5. **ABR lives in the client and is a control-theory problem.** Throughput-based, buffer-based (BOLA, with provable near-optimality via Lyapunov optimization), and hybrid controllers trade off average quality, rebuffering, and switching stability. Production players run buffer-based logic with a throughput safety floor.
6. **CDNs are the actual product for delivery.** Netflix Open Connect embeds appliances inside ISPs, proactively fills them nightly during off-peak windows, and achieves >95% cache hit ratios, serving the vast majority of bytes without touching AWS origin.
7. **The transport layer matters more than people expect.** HTTP-over-TCP won because it's cacheable and firewall-friendly; HTTP/3/QUIC now removes TCP head-of-line blocking and enables connection migration, which Netflix has adopted for the majority of streaming sessions.

## Details

### 1. Fundamentals: what VOD is and the core problem

**VOD vs live.** Video-on-demand means the entire asset exists before playback: it can be transcoded slowly, offline, at maximum quality, stored, and cached ahead of demand. Live streaming must encode and deliver in real time, so it trades compression efficiency and latency headroom for immediacy. The critical asymmetry: **VOD encodes once and reads millions of times**, which justifies spending enormous compute per title to shave bytes off every future stream. Live cannot look ahead (no two-pass, no per-title convex-hull search) and is dominated by glass-to-glass latency.

**The core problem.** You must deliver a large file over a network that is (a) lossy, (b) variable in bandwidth over time, and (c) terminating at wildly heterogeneous devices (a 4K TV on gigabit fiber vs a phone on congested cellular). A naive "download the MP4" approach fails on all three axes: one bitrate can't serve all devices, TCP throughput fluctuates, and a progressive download can't switch quality mid-stream. The entire VOD stack is the industry's answer.

**Digital video from first principles.** A frame is a 2D array of pixels; video is a time series of frames at some frame rate (24/25/30/60 fps). Each pixel has a color. Two representations matter:
- **RGB**: three color channels, natural for capture/display but redundant for compression because brightness and color are entangled.
- **YCbCr (a.k.a. YUV)**: separates luma (Y, brightness) from two chroma channels (Cb, Cr, color differences). This is the foundational trick, because the human visual system has far higher acuity for luminance than for chrominance.

**Chroma subsampling** exploits that asymmetry. Notation `J:a:b` describes a 4-wide reference block:
- **4:4:4**: no subsampling — full chroma resolution (used in high-end production, screen content).
- **4:2:2**: chroma sampled at half horizontal resolution (broadcast/pro).
- **4:2:0**: chroma sampled at half horizontal *and* half vertical resolution — chroma is 1/4 the samples. This is the streaming default; it cuts total sample count by 50% versus 4:4:4 with almost no perceptible loss. A 16×16 macroblock in 4:2:0 is 16×16 luma + 8×8 Cb + 8×8 Cr (four Y blocks, one Cb, one Cr at 8×8).

**Bit depth** (8/10/12 bits per component) sets tonal precision; 10-bit is standard for HDR and reduces banding. **Why raw video is enormous:** 1920×1080 × 1.5 bytes/pixel (4:2:0 8-bit) ≈ 3 MB/frame; ×60 fps ≈ 1.5 Gbps; a two-hour movie uncompressed is on the order of a terabyte-plus. Delivery over consumer networks is impossible without ~100–1000× compression, which is what codecs deliver.

### 2. Video compression and codecs

**The hybrid block-based coding model** (shared by H.26x, VP9, AV1) combines four ideas:

**(a) Intra-frame (spatial) compression** — the JPEG-like path. The frame is split into blocks (H.264 macroblocks of 16×16; HEVC Coding Tree Units up to 64×64; AV1 superblocks up to 128×128). Each block undergoes:
- **Prediction** from already-decoded neighbors (intra prediction).
- **Transform coding**: the residual is transformed via a DCT (or integer approximation — H.264 main profile uses 4×4 transform blocks, high profile 4×4 or 8×8) into frequency coefficients, concentrating energy in low frequencies.
- **Quantization**: coefficients are divided by a quantization step (controlled by QP) and rounded — *this is where loss and compression happen*. High frequencies (fine detail the eye is forgiving of) get quantized away.
- **Entropy coding**: the quantized coefficients are losslessly packed via CABAC/CAVLC (H.264) or arithmetic coding.

**(b) Inter-frame (temporal) compression** — the big win for video. Most frames resemble their neighbors, so instead of coding pixels, you code *motion*:
- **I-frames** (intra): self-contained, decodable alone, the random-access points. Largest.
- **P-frames** (predicted): reference a previous frame via **motion vectors** — the encoder searches for the best-matching block in a reference frame (motion estimation), and codes only the motion vector + residual (motion compensation). Modern codecs use variable-sized prediction partitions, each with its own motion vector.
- **B-frames** (bidirectional): reference both past and future frames, highest compression, but introduce reordering (decode order ≠ display order, which is why fMP4 `trun` boxes carry composition-time offsets).
- **GOP (Group of Pictures)**: the I-frame-to-I-frame structure (e.g., IBBPBBP…). GOP length trades compression (longer = smaller) against seekability and error resilience (shorter = more random-access points). **Segment boundaries in streaming must align with closed GOPs / IDR frames** so each segment is independently decodable.

**Why this matters for VOD:** the codec determines bytes-per-quality, which directly sets CDN egress cost (the dominant delivery expense) and storage. Better codecs also enable higher resolutions within the same bandwidth envelope.

**Codec comparison:**
| Codec | Year | Efficiency vs H.264 | Encode cost | Licensing |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| H.264/AVC | 2003 | baseline | cheapest, fastest | one pool (Via LA), modest |
| H.265/HEVC | 2013 | ~50% at 4K | ~3–5× H.264 | fragmented, multiple pools |
| VP9 | 2013 | ~HEVC-class | ~8× H.264 | royalty-free (Google) |
| AV1 | 2018 | ~30% better than HEVC | historically 10–50× H.264 | royalty-free (AOMedia) |
| VVC/H.266 | 2020 | ~50% better than HEVC | ~3× AV1 | fragmented, streaming-absent |

HEVC uses larger, recursively-split CTUs and better motion prediction than H.264's fixed macroblocks. AV1 adds flexible partitioning (4×4 to 128×128), advanced prediction, and loop filters (CDEF, loop restoration). Compression gains grow with resolution — one MDPI study found AV1's advantage over H.264 ranges from ~33% at FHD to ~81% at 8K, with HEVC-vs-H.264 savings from ~14% at FHD to ~77% at 8K, and VVC edging AV1 at the highest resolutions.

**Licensing is the decisive real-world factor.** HEVC is split across multiple patent pools (Access Advance/HEVC Advance listing 29,000+ patents, the former Via LA program, plus unaffiliated holders), making total cost unpredictable; this is widely credited with keeping HEVC off the open web (browsers other than Safari/Edge never added it, and YouTube avoids it). AV1 is royalty-free under AOMedia, whose members include Google, Netflix, Apple, Microsoft, Amazon, Meta, Nvidia, Intel, ARM, Samsung, Cisco, and Mozilla. Note the contested nuance: Sisvel, Avanci, and Access Advance have since launched patent pools asserting rights against AV1/VP9, so "royalty-free" is legally contested even if AOMedia grants free licenses. VVC repeats HEVC's fragmentation and is ~3× more expensive to encode than AV1, so it has essentially no mainstream streaming deployment (its traction is in broadcast — ATSC 3.0 added it July 2025, Brazil's TV 3.0 launched over-the-air VVC in August 2025).

### 3. Encoding & transcoding pipeline

**Ingest → transcode → package.** A high-quality mezzanine/source is ingested, validated, then transcoded into **multiple renditions** — the **bitrate ladder** (a set of resolution/bitrate pairs). The reason for multiple renditions is ABR: the client needs a menu of qualities to switch between as bandwidth varies.

**Per-title and per-shot encoding.** Netflix's original fixed ladder (a one-size-fits-all set of resolution/bitrate pairs chosen when it deployed H.264/AVC encodes in late 2010) wasted bits on easy content (animation) and starved hard content (film grain, sports). Per-title encoding computes, per title, the **convex hull** of quality vs bitrate — brute-forcing many encodes at many resolution/bitrate points and picking the Pareto-optimal ("best quality at each bitrate") rungs. Netflix later moved to **per-shot / dynamic optimization**, doing this analysis per shot. Quality is measured with **VMAF** (Video Multimethod Assessment Fusion), a perceptual metric Netflix co-developed with USC and open-sourced (replacing PSNR in its decision engine around June 2016), fusing multiple quality signals including a motion component; it correlates with subjective DMOS far better than PSNR. A rule of thumb: **6 VMAF points ≈ 1 JND (just-noticeable difference)**, perceived by ~75% of viewers. Per the Netflix TechBlog (Katsavounidis), dynamic optimization "enabled Netflix to reduce the bitrate of x264, VP9 (libvpx), and x265 by 28.04%, 37.61%, and 33.51%, respectively, while retaining the same quality."

**Rate control modes** (the outer loop deciding bits per frame/scene/title):
- **CBR**: constant bitrate; wastes bits on easy scenes, protects the network; used for legacy/broadcast/live where a stable pipe matters. CBR's flat output aligns with congestion-control loops and prevents queue buildup at bottlenecks.
- **VBR**: targets an average bitrate, lets instantaneous rate vary; better quality distribution. A 2× peak-to-average ratio on a 4 Mbps target can burst to 8 Mbps.
- **CRF (Constant Rate Factor)**: fixes a *quality* target (0–51 for H.26x; 0–63 for AV1), lets bitrate float. CRF 23 is the x264 default; visually lossless around CRF 18; CRF 28 is the x265 default; SVT-AV1 ~CRF 30 ≈ x264 CRF 23 at ~40–50% lower bitrate. Great for archival, bad for delivery predictability (you don't know the resulting bitrate).
- **Capped CRF**: CRF quality target with a max-bitrate ceiling (VBV) — the practical per-title workhorse: behaves like CRF on easy scenes (saving bandwidth), clamps like CBR on hard scenes (protecting delivery). This is what most VOD ladders ship (`-crf 22 -maxrate 5M -bufsize 10M`).
- **Two-pass**: first pass measures complexity, second allocates bits — meaningfully better *when hitting a fixed bitrate target* (1–3 VMAF at constrained bitrates), unnecessary for CRF (nothing to plan against), and impossible for live (no lookahead). Beyond two passes, gains are negligible.

**VBV (Video Buffering Verifier)** constrains bitrate to a client buffer model via `-maxrate`/`-bufsize` (a good default is bufsize = 2× maxrate), ensuring the stream never exceeds what a given buffer can absorb. Note the x264 developers' own guidance to avoid pure single-pass ABR — it guesses at bit allocation and produces large quality swings within short segments, which is exactly what hurts HTTP adaptive streaming.

**Hardware vs software encoding.** Software (x264/x265/libaom/SVT-AV1) gives best compression but is slow (on a 16-core CPU at 1080p, roughly: x264 ~120 fps, x265 ~30 fps, libvpx-VP9 ~15 fps, SVT-AV1 preset 5 ~10 fps). Hardware (GPU NVENC, Apple VideoToolbox, or custom ASICs) trades some efficiency for speed/throughput. Because YouTube ingests an enormous volume of video (Google states "more than 500 hours of video content is uploaded to YouTube every minute") that is mostly watched few times, it built the **Argos VCU (Video Coding Unit)** ASIC, detailed in Google's ASPLOS 2021 paper "Warehouse-Scale Video Acceleration"; per Google engineer Jeff Calow, it delivers "up to 20-33x improvements in compute efficiency compared to our previous optimized system, which was running software on traditional servers" (a TCO figure over a three-year period). A production machine holds 20 VCUs (10 PCIe cards × 2 Argos chips), each chip with 10 encoder cores that can each encode 2160p in real time up to 60 fps. Netflix, encoding comparatively few titles watched millions of times, spends software compute lavishly on convex-hull search across hundreds of parallel EC2 instances (its microservices video pipeline runs on the Cosmos platform, splitting long encodes into chunks processed in parallel).

### 4. Packaging & container formats

**Container formats** wrap coded elementary streams with timing/metadata:
- **MPEG-TS**: legacy transport stream (188-byte packets), historically required by HLS.
- **ISOBMFF / MP4**: the ISO Base Media File Format (ISO/IEC 14496-12) — a tree of **boxes (atoms)**. Key boxes: `ftyp` (brand/compatibility), `moov` (movie metadata: tracks via `trak`, codec config, and sample tables like `stco`/`stsz`/`stsc` that let you compute any frame's byte offset), `mdat` (the actual coded samples, stored unframed — no start codes). "Fast-start" moves `moov` before `mdat` so playback can begin without downloading the whole file.
- **Fragmented MP4 (fMP4)**: instead of one big `moov`+`mdat`, the file is broken into an init segment (`ftyp`+`moov` with zero-sample-count tracks) followed by repeated **`moof`+`mdat`** fragment pairs. `moof` (movie fragment box) carries per-fragment metadata (`mfhd` sequence number, `traf`→`tfhd`/`tfdt` decode time/`trun` sample runs); `mdat` carries the samples. Typically each GOP is one `moof`/`mdat` pair, fragmented at keyframes. For a small single-frame fragment the box overhead can be ~96 bytes — enough that the IETF is drafting compressed-box-header schemes for low-latency use.
- **CMAF (Common Media Application Format, ISO/IEC 23000-19)**: not a new container but a *constrained* fMP4 profile — single track per segment, defined timing structure, and a standardized encryption format — so the exact same segment bytes work in both HLS and DASH. Structurally: one `init.mp4` (ftyp+moov) + a sequence of `.m4s` media segments, each a self-contained `moof`+`mdat`.
- **WebM**: Matroska-based, for VP9/AV1 (used by YouTube on the web).

**Why segmentation is necessary.** Chopping the stream into 2–6 second segments (each starting with an IDR frame) is what makes everything else possible:
- **Adaptive switching**: the player can pick a different rendition at each segment boundary.
- **HTTP cacheability**: each segment is a normal, immutable HTTP object a CDN can cache.
- **Random access / seeking**: jump to the segment containing the target time.
- **Parallelism**: encode/transcode segments independently across a fleet.

### 5. Adaptive Bitrate Streaming (ABR) — the heart of the system

**The model.** The server hosts multiple renditions, each segmented, plus a **manifest** describing them. The client downloads the manifest, then pulls segments one at a time over HTTP, choosing a rendition per segment based on measured conditions. All intelligence is client-side, which is exactly why HTTP + dumb static servers/CDNs suffice — a huge architectural simplification over stateful streaming servers, and why DASH can be served from a plain static file server.

**HLS (RFC 8216, Apple, 2009; standardized as IETF Informational RFC in August 2017).** Playlists are UTF-8 `.m3u8` text files derived from the M3U format. Two tiers:
- **Multivariant (master) playlist**: lists variant streams via `EXT-X-STREAM-INF` (with `BANDWIDTH`, `RESOLUTION`, `CODECS`), each pointing to a media playlist.
- **Media playlist**: lists segments with `EXTINF` durations, `EXT-X-TARGETDURATION`, `EXT-X-MEDIA-SEQUENCE`; `EXT-X-ENDLIST` marks a complete VOD asset (vs a live playlist that keeps refreshing); `EXT-X-KEY` signals encryption; `EXT-X-MAP` points to the init segment for fMP4.
```
#EXTM3U
#EXT-X-STREAM-INF:BANDWIDTH=5042000,RESOLUTION=1920x1080
1080.m3u8
#EXT-X-STREAM-INF:BANDWIDTH=861000,RESOLUTION=640x360
360.m3u8
```
HLS is now tracked in `draft-pantos-hls-rfc8216bis` (the active revision as of mid-2026 describes protocol version 13); it originally mandated MPEG-TS but now supports fMP4/CMAF (version 6+), with an fMP4 init segment requiring an `iso6`-or-higher `ftyp`.

**MPEG-DASH (ISO/IEC 23009-1, current fifth edition 2022).** The manifest is the **MPD** (Media Presentation Description), an XML four-level hierarchy: **Presentation → Period(s) → AdaptationSet(s) → Representation(s) → Segments**. A Period is a time slot (used for ads/chapters); an AdaptationSet groups interchangeable tracks (all video renditions, or one audio language); a Representation is one coded version (fixed bitrate/resolution/codec). Segment addressing uses `SegmentTemplate` with `$Number$` (default VOD) or `$Time$`+`SegmentTimeline` (variable-duration/live), or byte-range access via `SegmentBase`+`indexRange` into a single file. DASH is codec- and (outside Apple) device-agnostic and natively supports multi-DRM via CENC (Widevine + PlayReady PSSH boxes in one MPD).

**HLS vs DASH.** HLS is mandatory on Apple devices and carries the majority of long-form on-demand minutes on the public internet; DASH is the open standard everywhere outside Apple and has cleaner multi-DRM. The industry answer to "which?" is increasingly "both, over shared CMAF segments" — encode/store once, reference from both an m3u8 and an mpd. A common production bug: declaring a fixed segment duration in the manifest while the encoder emits slightly-off durations (GOP closing early), causing drift; fix by pinning GOP-aligned segments or using `SegmentTimeline`.

**ABR algorithms — the control problem.** The player optimizes a QoE utility that rewards high average bitrate and penalizes rebuffering and excessive switching. Three families:
- **Throughput-based (rate-based)**: estimate future bandwidth from recent segment download speeds (often dual EWMA — a fast ~2s half-life and a slow one), pick the highest rendition that fits. Reacts fast but is fooled by noisy/bursty throughput and can over-switch.
- **Buffer-based**: drive the decision off buffer occupancy. **BOLA** (Spiteri et al., IEEE INFOCOM 2016) formalizes this as Lyapunov optimization, proving utility "within an additive term O(1/V) of the optimal value" *without predicting bandwidth*; it maximizes `utility(rendition) − γ·(buffer cost)` at each segment boundary. BOLA is the default ABR in dash.js. Its weakness: because buffer integrates throughput over time, it reacts slowly to sudden bandwidth crashes.
- **Hybrid**: production players run buffer-based logic (BOLA-like) with a throughput safety floor — e.g., downshift immediately if measured throughput drops below ~50% of current rendition bitrate for several segments. dash.js's DYNAMIC algorithm blends BOLA and throughput rules; the FAST SWITCHING technique replaces already-buffered low-quality segments with higher-quality ones. Netflix published a buffer-based approach in 2013.
- **ML-based**: **Pensieve** (Mao, Netravali & Alizadeh, MIT, SIGCOMM 2017) trains an ABR policy with reinforcement learning (A3C), learning from observed outcomes rather than fixed heuristics; the paper reports "In all considered scenarios, Pensieve outperforms the best state-of-the-art scheme, with improvements in average QoE of 12%–25%."

**The core trade-offs.** Bigger buffers reduce rebuffering but increase startup latency and waste bytes on abandoned sessions; aggressive up-switching raises quality but risks rebuffers; frequent switching hurts perceived quality. **Startup latency** is minimized by starting at a low rendition and ramping up, and by fetching the smallest first segment fast. QoE metrics that matter: startup time, rebuffer ratio, average bitrate/VMAF, and switching frequency.

### 6. Content Delivery Networks (CDNs)

**Why CDNs are essential.** Serving petabytes to millions of concurrent viewers from a central origin is impossible on latency, bandwidth, and cost. CDNs push content to **edge servers / PoPs** near users. Because HTTP-based streaming segments are immutable, cacheable objects, standard HTTP caching works — this is the deep reason the industry moved to HTTP streaming. Cache hits at the edge serve most requests; misses pull from a caching hierarchy (edge → regional/mid-tier → origin). Eviction is typically LRU-class.

**Request routing.** Users are steered to a good edge via **DNS-based routing** (resolve a hostname to a nearby PoP), **anycast** (same IP announced from many locations, BGP routes to the nearest), and **geo/latency routing**. Application-layer steering (a control service handing the client a ranked list of endpoints) gives finer control than DNS TTL games.

**Netflix Open Connect — the case study.** Netflix built its own CDN rather than renting one:
- **OCAs (Open Connect Appliances)** are storage-dense servers running a lightly-customized FreeBSD (development branch, chosen partly for its permissive license) plus NGINX and the BIRD routing daemon, placed *inside ISP networks* (embedded) or at IXPs. Netflix gives them to qualifying ISPs for free; the ISP provides rack space, power, and a BGP session. Netflix's official specs list a 2U storage appliance at up to 120 TB raw and ~200 Gbps, and a "global appliance" at up to 60 TB / ~80 Gbps. (A secondary CDN-vendor blog claims a 2026-generation 560 TB NVMe appliance, but this is not on Netflix's official spec page — treat as unverified.)
- **Proactive fill.** Because Netflix knows its catalog and predicts demand, OCAs are filled **nightly during off-peak "fill windows"** (shiftable ±2h by the ISP), coordinated by a control plane running in AWS that builds a per-OCA manifest; each OCA compares its catalog to the manifest, deletes what's no longer needed, and downloads what's missing. New releases are pre-positioned hours before global launch. This converts cache misses from a latency event into a capacity-planning problem; Netflix has cited hit ratios exceeding ~95% in well-provisioned embedded deployments.
- **Storage & origin.** The master catalog lives in AWS S3; OCAs sync a subset via a fill hierarchy (peer-fill between co-located OCAs in a cluster, then IXP, then transit/origin). Steering uses BGP path metrics — routes reported by embedded OCAs have shorter AS-path length, and Netflix manipulates MED values to prefer direct-peering paths for OCA communications. Reaching 100 Gbps per FreeBSD appliance required in-kernel TLS and NUMA-aware `sendfile` engineering (Netflix added TLS to content delivery in 2016; ~10–20% of the most popular titles are served from memory, the rest from disk).
- **Scale.** Netflix has spent over $1B and deployed thousands of OCAs across 100+ countries; it accounts for a large share of downstream internet traffic in many markets. During the 2026 Super Bowl, per a BlazingCDN analysis (secondary/vendor source), Netflix "sustained over 18 Tbps of aggregate egress from its Open Connect footprint" with "virtually none of that traffic [touching] Netflix's origin in AWS."

**Cost.** CDN egress is the dominant delivery cost, which is why better codecs (fewer bytes) and ISP-embedded caching (avoiding transit) directly hit the bottom line.

### 7. DRM & content protection

**From first principles.** Content owners require that the decrypted content and the keys never be trivially extractable. The scheme:
- **Encryption**: content is encrypted at packaging with AES, using a **Content Encryption Key (CEK)** per track/rendition. **Common Encryption (CENC, ISO/IEC 23001-7)** defines two modes: **`cenc`** = AES-CTR (counter mode, used by Widevine/PlayReady) and **`cbcs`** = AES-CBC with pattern encryption (used by FairPlay). CENC encrypts per-track so keys stay valid across ABR renditions — DRM doesn't break when bitrate switches.
- **License/key exchange**: the encrypted content ships with key identifiers (PSSH boxes / `EXT-X-KEY`). At playback, the player asks a **license server** for the key; the server authenticates entitlement (subscription, device, geo, output rules — often via a signed JWT/session token) and returns a license containing the CEK, wrapped so only the device's secure module can use it. Sub-500ms license latency is the target for smooth startup.
- **CDM (Content Decryption Module)**: the black box in the device/browser that holds keys and decrypts. **The key never touches JavaScript.**

**The three DRM systems** and their reach: **Widevine** (Google — Chrome, Android, many TVs; security levels L1 hardware / L3 software), **PlayReady** (Microsoft — Windows, Xbox, many TVs/STBs; SL3000/SL2000), **FairPlay** (Apple — Safari/iOS/tvOS; requires `cbcs`). Security levels gate resolution (hardware DRM for 4K, software near SD). Together they cover ~99% of devices; premium services run **multi-DRM**.

**EME (Encrypted Media Extensions)** is the W3C browser API that brokers between the player (JavaScript), the CDM, and the license server, without plugins. Flow: player queries available key systems → `requestMediaKeySystemAccess` (e.g., `com.widevine.alpha`) → CDM generates a license challenge → player relays it to the license server → server returns license → `session.update()` installs the key → CDM decrypts. MSE feeds the encrypted segments; EME handles decryption transparently.

**The CMAF+`cbcs` insight:** encrypt once with `cbcs` and the same encrypted segments are consumable by all three DRMs (FairPlay via HLS manifest, Widevine/PlayReady via DASH manifest), each using its own license flow. This is a major operational simplification — one encode, one encryption, three DRMs, storage cost unchanged from single-DRM. (Caveat: some legacy CTV/STB clients only accept `cenc`, so check playback analytics before going `cbcs`-only.)

**AES-128 vs SAMPLE-AES:** HLS's original `AES-128` encrypts the entire segment; **SAMPLE-AES** encrypts only the media samples inside the container (leaving headers parseable), which is what enables DRM-aware sample-level encryption and the CENC schemes.

### 8. The player / client side

A modern web player is built on two browser APIs:
- **MSE (Media Source Extensions)**: replaces a simple `<video src=…>` with a `MediaSource` object exposing `SourceBuffer`s. JavaScript fetches segments (fetch/XHR → ArrayBuffer) and calls `appendBuffer()` to feed the decode pipeline, giving fine control over buffering and adaptation. The player appends the init segment first (codec config — the SourceBuffer MIME type must match, e.g. `video/mp4; codecs="avc1.4d401f"`), then media segments; the browser demuxes, decodes, and renders. MSE tracks per-track buffers, uses presentation/decode timestamps and an append window to place coded frames, and exposes a `HAVE_ENOUGH_DATA` readiness state; the player watches `SourceBuffer.updating` before further appends. (Chrome 108+ even allows MSE in web workers for performance.)
- **EME**: handles the encrypted path as above.

**The pipeline inside:** network fetch → source buffer → **demux** (parse fMP4 boxes into coded frames) → **decode** (ideally hardware-accelerated: dedicated silicon for H.264/HEVC/AV1) → render/composite → display. A separate **decode buffer** absorbs jitter between the network and the decoder. In engine terms (e.g., GStreamer-based WebKit ports), this is realized as N "append pipelines" (one per SourceBuffer, each with appsrc → demux → parser) feeding one playback pipeline.

**Handling variability & seeking.** Because segments align on IDR frames (random-access points), the player can switch renditions cleanly at boundaries (append the next segment from a different quality), and can **seek** by jumping to the segment containing the target time and starting decode from the nearest random-access point. **Startup latency** is minimized by fetching a small low-bitrate first segment and a small initial buffer, then ramping. Fast-switching techniques replace already-buffered low-quality segments with higher-quality ones when bandwidth improves.

### 9. Networking & protocols

**Why HTTP won.** Older streaming used stateful push protocols (RTMP over TCP, RTSP/RTP). HTTP-based streaming beat them because: (a) segments are cacheable by the entire existing web/CDN infrastructure, (b) it traverses firewalls/NAT on ports 80/443, (c) it needs only dumb static servers, and (d) adaptation logic moves to the client. RTMP survives mostly as an *ingest* protocol.

**HTTP/1.1 → HTTP/2 → HTTP/3.** HTTP/1.1 does one request at a time per connection. HTTP/2 multiplexes many streams over one TCP connection — but suffers **TCP head-of-line blocking**: because TCP presents a single in-order byte stream, one lost packet stalls *all* multiplexed streams while the kernel waits for retransmission, even though the other streams' bytes have arrived intact. **HTTP/3 (RFC 9114, June 2022) runs on QUIC (RFC 9000) over UDP**, implementing streams in the transport layer so a lost packet stalls only its own stream; it also folds the TLS 1.3 handshake into connection setup (1-RTT vs TCP+TLS's ~3, plus 0-RTT resumption) and supports **connection migration** (survive a Wi-Fi-to-cellular change via PATH_CHALLENGE/PATH_RESPONSE). QUIC also moves congestion control into user space, so new algorithms ship without kernel/OS updates. Caveat: QUIC congestion control is still per-connection, so streams share one controller and loss on any stream still affects the aggregate rate.

**TCP behavior & congestion control.** TCP's congestion control (Cubic, BBR) probes for bandwidth and backs off on loss; its sawtooth throughput is exactly the variability ABR must smooth over. **TLS** is now universal for streaming. Netflix's client stack now uses QUIC for the majority of streaming sessions, improving mobile connection migration and tail latency during congestion.

### 10. Storage & infrastructure

**Object storage is the origin of record.** Massive catalogs live in object storage (Amazon S3 for Netflix) rather than block/file storage because object storage offers a flat, HTTP-addressable namespace, near-infinite horizontal scale, and extreme durability for write-once/read-many immutable media (and, on AWS, free S3→CloudFront egress). S3 is designed for **11 nines (99.999999999%) durability**, achieved via redundant storage across 3+ availability zones, erasure coding (data + parity shards — e.g., a 14+6 Reed-Solomon-style scheme gives ~1.43× overhead vs 3× for full replication, the exact scheme being an informed estimate not an AWS disclosure), checksums, and background scrubbing. Per the AWS News Blog "Twenty years of Amazon S3" (March 2026), "S3 stores more than 500 trillion objects and serves more than 200 million requests per second globally across hundreds of exabytes of data in 123 Availability Zones in 39 AWS Regions."

**Scale.** A single Netflix title is transcoded into dozens of renditions (multiple codecs × resolutions × bitrates × audio tracks × subtitle tracks); it is commonly reported in engineering analyses that this produces on the order of 1,000+ files per title once segmented (Netflix's TechBlog confirms the *methodology* but not this specific count — treat as illustrative). Encoding runs elastically across hundreds of EC2 instances. Per AWS re:Invent 2025 (session STG214), "Netflix manages exabytes of content and data across AWS"; its studio media platform tracked ~14.5 billion file/nodes as of late 2024, adding ~50 million per week. YouTube ingests ~500 hours of video per minute, processed through its Argos VCU ASIC fleet (thousands of chips in production).

**Overall architecture of a large VOD service:** ingest → cloud transcode farm (per-title/per-shot, VMAF-driven) → packager (CMAF segmentation + CENC encryption) → object-storage origin → CDN (proactive fill to edge/embedded appliances) → client (manifest → ABR over MSE → decrypt via EME → hardware decode → render). Control planes (steering, licensing, catalog, telemetry) run separately in the cloud.

### 11. Advanced & emerging topics (2024–2026)

- **Low-latency delivery.** **Chunked CMAF** breaks each segment into small (100–400 ms) `moof`+`mdat` chunks emitted and delivered via **HTTP chunked transfer encoding** as they're encoded, so the player decodes before the full segment exists (a chunk that starts with a Stream Access Point is "independent"). **LL-HLS** (Apple, 2020) exposes these as partial segments (`EXT-X-PART`), preload hints, blocking playlist reloads, and delta playlists; **LL-DASH** streams chunks with `availabilityTimeOffset`. This cuts glass-to-glass latency from 15–45s (standard) to ~2–5s. The catch: chunked transfer breaks the classic ABR bandwidth-estimation trick (segment-arrival-time no longer implies bandwidth, since data arrives at encoding pace), requiring new estimators; and CDNs must forward partial chunks without buffering.
- **AI/ML in the pipeline.** RL-based ABR (Pensieve and successors like Comyco and PPO-ABR), ML-driven per-scene encoding decisions, and content-aware encoding are moving from research to production.
- **Super-resolution upscaling.** Client-side neural upscaling reconstructs higher resolution from lower-bitrate streams, trading device compute for bandwidth.
- **AV1 at scale.** AV1 hardware decode is now widespread (iPhone 15 Pro+, M3+ Macs, recent Snapdragon/Dimensity, most 2022+ TVs). Per Netflix's December 2025 TechBlog ("AV1 — Now Powering 30% of Netflix Streaming"): "Over the past five years (2021–2025), 88% of large-screen devices…submitted for Netflix certification have supported AV1"; AV1 is now "approximately 30% of all Netflix streaming…our second most-used codec," with sessions using one-third less bandwidth, 45% fewer buffering interruptions, and VMAF 4.3 points higher than AVC. Netflix also deployed AV1 **Film Grain Synthesis** (July 2025) to encode grain as a parameter rather than expensive noise. SVT-AV1 (Intel+Netflix), designed for multi-threaded/tiled execution, narrowed the encode-speed gap dramatically.
- **QUIC/HTTP-3 adoption.** Now used for the majority of streaming sessions on some large services (including Netflix), improving mobile connection migration and tail latency.
- **Newer codecs beyond AV1:** VVC/H.266 (broadcast-focused so far — ATSC 3.0, Brazil TV 3.0), and AOMedia's next-generation successor to AV1 in development.

## Recommendations

**If you're building a VOD service, stage your decisions like this:**

1. **Start with HTTP + CMAF + HLS/DASH.** Encode a bitrate ladder, package once as CMAF fMP4 segments (2–6s), generate both an m3u8 and an mpd from the same bytes. This is the lowest-risk, highest-compatibility foundation. Benchmark that changes the call: if you're Apple-only, HLS alone suffices; if you need sub-5s latency, jump to chunked CMAF + LL-HLS/LL-DASH (and confirm your CDN forwards partial chunks and supports HTTP/2 preload hints).
2. **Codec strategy: ship H.264 as the universal floor, add AV1 for your top device tiers.** Use H.264 for the lowest ladder rungs and legacy devices; add AV1 where hardware decode exists to cut egress ~30–50%. Only consider HEVC if your device matrix is TV-heavy and you've priced the patent-pool exposure. Skip VVC for streaming today. **Threshold to add AV1 broadly:** when >80–90% of your watch-time is on AV1-hardware-capable devices (Netflix crossed this on large screens — 88% of certified large-screen devices — before making AV1 ~30% of streaming).
3. **Rate control: use capped CRF per title.** It gives per-title-encoding benefits with almost any FFmpeg-based encoder. If you have scale and titles watched many times, invest in convex-hull per-title/per-shot optimization driven by VMAF. **Threshold:** per-shot optimization pays off when (encode compute cost) << (bandwidth saved × views); obviously worth it above millions of views/title (Netflix's ~28–38% published savings), rarely worth it below thousands.
4. **ABR: adopt a mature player (dash.js/hls.js/Shaka) with hybrid buffer+throughput logic; don't roll your own.** Tune buffer targets to your latency goal. Only invest in ML-based ABR once you've exhausted tuning and have the telemetry to train on.
5. **CDN: rent commercial CDN first; build your own only at extreme scale.** Netflix's Open Connect made sense at a large fraction of internet traffic; for everyone else, a commercial CDN with volume pricing captures most of the benefit. **Threshold to consider embedded/custom CDN:** sustained multi-Tbps egress where transit + commercial CDN bills exceed the amortized cost of building.
6. **DRM: if you need it, encrypt once with `cbcs` CMAF and use a multi-DRM SaaS.** Don't run three license servers yourself. Only add DRM if content licensing requires it — it adds real operational complexity (per-DRM certs, key rotation, `cbcs`-vs-`cenc` legacy-device edge cases).
7. **Transport: enable HTTP/2 and HTTP/3 at the CDN.** HTTP/3/QUIC especially helps mobile and lossy networks (per-stream loss isolation, connection migration); it's a low-risk win once your CDN supports it.

## Caveats

- **Vendor-blog bias.** Much of the accessible detail comes from vendor engineering blogs (Mux, Bitmovin, Fora Soft, CDN vendors) that have commercial incentives; where possible this report anchors to primary specs (RFC 8216, RFC 9000/9114, ISO/IEC 23009-1 DASH, ISO/IEC 23000-19 CMAF, ISO/IEC 23001-7 CENC, ISO/IEC 14496-12 ISOBMFF, W3C MSE/EME) and academic/primary papers (BOLA INFOCOM 2016, Pensieve SIGCOMM 2017, Google's Argos ASPLOS 2021, Netflix TechBlog).
- **Some scale figures are secondary/estimated.** "~1,000+ files per Netflix title," the claimed 560 TB OCA, and the 18 Tbps Super Bowl figure circulate in engineering/vendor analyses rather than Netflix primary docs; Netflix's official OCA spec page (up to 120 TB storage appliance), TechBlog, and AWS re:Invent sessions are the authoritative sources. Treat specific per-title file counts and the 560 TB claim as illustrative/unverified.
- **Numbers move.** Codec efficiency percentages depend heavily on content, resolution, and encoder settings/presets — the "50% better" figures are directional, not guarantees (the MDPI cross-resolution study shows savings widening dramatically from FHD to 8K). AV1 encode-speed and hardware-support figures are improving rapidly, so any 2026 snapshot will age.
- **Competing perspectives are real.** HLS vs DASH, AV1 vs HEVC vs VVC, and "royalty-free" AV1's actual patent exposure (Sisvel, Avanci, and Access Advance have asserted pools against AV1/VP9) are genuinely contested; some commentators argue HEVC's licensing was "misunderstood" rather than broken. The report reflects the mainstream streaming-industry consensus, but reasonable engineers disagree on codec bets and CDN build-vs-buy.
- **Live is a different animal.** This report is VOD-focused; live/low-latency changes many trade-offs (no lookahead, tighter latency budgets, WebRTC for sub-second use cases), noted only where they intersect VOD.
- **"ISCA vs ASPLOS."** Google's Argos/VCU paper "Warehouse-Scale Video Acceleration" was published at ASPLOS 2021 (not ISCA); the 20–33× figure is an efficiency/TCO number over a three-year period, not raw throughput.
